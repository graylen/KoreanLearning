Level 01 Lesson 1 Lesson Notes

When you want to say hello:

안녕하세요. [an-nyeong-ha-se-yo.] Hello.
안녕 [an-nyeong] peace / well-being
하세요 [ha-se-yo] please do (polite)
When you want to say thank you:

감사합니다. [gam-sa-ham-ni-da.] Thank you.
감사 [gam-sa] gratitude
합니다 [ham-ni-da] I do (formal and respectful) 

Level 01 Lesson 2 Lesson Notes

When you want to say yes or show agreement:
 

네. [ne.] Yes. / I agree. / That’s right.
(Also used as: I see / Got it / Uh-huh / What? depending on tone)
 
네, 맞아요. [ne, ma-ja-yo.] Yes, that’s right.
When you want to say no or show disagreement:
 

아니요. [a-ni-yo.] No. / I don’t agree. / That’s not right.
When agreeing with a negative question:
 

네, 안 좋아해요. [ne, an jo-a-hae-yo.] Yes, I don’t like it.
When disagreeing with a negative question:
 

아니요, 좋아해요. [a-ni-yo, jo-a-hae-yo.] No, I like it.
When answering a positive question:
 

네, 좋아해요. [ne, jo-a-hae-yo.] Yes, I like it.
 
아니요, 안 좋아해요. [a-ni-yo, an jo-a-hae-yo.] No, I don’t like it.

Level 01 Lesson 3 Lesson Notes

When you are leaving and the other person is staying:

안녕히 계세요. [an-nyeong-hi gye-se-yo.] Goodbye. (Stay in peace.)
안녕히 [an-nyeong-hi] in peace / peacefully / safely
계세요. [gye-se-yo.] Stay.
When the other person is leaving (and you are staying), or when both of you are leaving:

안녕히 가세요. [an-nyeong-hi ga-se-yo.] Goodbye. (Go in peace.)
안녕히 [an-nyeong-hi] in peace / peacefully / safely
가세요. [ga-se-yo.] Please go.

Level 01 Lesson 6 Lesson Notes

When you want to say “This is [noun]”:

이거 [i-geo] this, this one
이 + 것 = 이것 → 이거 (spoken form)
Use -이에요 after a consonant:

책 [chaek] book
이거 책이에요. [i-geo chae-gi-e-yo.] This is a book.
사전 [sa-jeon] dictionary
이거 사전이에요. [i-geo sa-jeo-ni-e-yo.] This is a dictionary.
Use -예요 after a vowel:

카메라 [ka-me-ra] camera
이거 카메라예요. [i-geo ka-me-ra-ye-yo.] This is a camera.
커피 [keo-pi] coffee
이거 커피예요. [i-geo keo-pi-ye-yo.] This is coffee.
To ask “What is this?”:

뭐 [mwo] what
이거 뭐예요? [i-geo mwo-ye-yo?] What is this?
To ask “Is this [noun]?”, raise your tone:

이거 커피예요? [i-geo keo-pi-ye-yo?] Is this coffee?
To answer:

네, 맞아요. [ne, ma-ja-yo.] Yes, that's right.
아니요. [a-ni-yo.] No.
이거 물이에요. [i-geo mu-ri-e-yo.] This is water.

Level 01 Lesson 7 Lesson Notes

When referring to objects based on location:

이 [i] this (near the speaker)
그 [geu] that (near the listener)
저 [jeo] that (far from both)
Used before nouns:

이 책 [i chaek] this book
그 사람 [geu sa-ram] that person
저 카메라 [jeo ka-me-ra] that camera over there
To say “this/that/it” as a pronoun, add:

것 [geot] thing
Combined forms:

이것 [i-geot] this thing
그것 [geu-geot] that thing
저것 [jeo-geot] that thing over there
Spoken (simplified) forms:

이거 [i-geo] this thing
그거 [geu-geo] that thing / it
저거 [jeo-geo] that thing over there
거 = casual form of 것

These are for objects or ideas, not people.
For people, use 사람 [sa-ram] person:

이 사람 [i sa-ram] this person
그 사람 [geu sa-ram] that person
저 사람 [jeo sa-ram] that person over there

Level 01 Lesson 9 Lesson Notes

Topic markers show what the speaker is talking about:

-은 [-eun] after consonant
-는 [-neun] after vowel
Key Expressions

이것은 [i-geo-seun] as for this
이거는 [i-geo-neun] as for this (spoken)
이건 [i-geon] as for this (more casual)
저는 [jeo-neun] as for me
저는 학생이에요. [jeo-neun hak-ssaeng-i-e-yo.] I am a student.
이것은 가방이에요. [i-geo-seun ga-bang-i-e-yo.] This is a bag.
Subject markers show who or what performs the action:

-이 [-i] after consonant
-가 [-ga] after vowel
Examples

이것이 [i-geo-si] this thing (subject)
이게 [i-ge] this thing (subject, spoken)
이것이 가방이에요. [i-geo-si ga-bang-i-e-yo.] This is a bag.
Compare:

이것은 가방이에요. [i-geo-seun ga-bang-i-e-yo.] (Introducing: This is a bag.)
이것이 가방이에요. [i-geo-si ga-bang-i-e-yo.] (Emphasizing: It is this one that is a bag.)
저는 학생이에요. [jeo-neun hak-ssaeng-i-e-yo.] I am a student.
제가 학생이에요. [je-ga hak-ssaeng-i-e-yo.] I am a student. (answering “Who is a student?”)
저 + 가 → 제가 [je-ga] I (subject)

Particles may change depending on sound and formality, and are often omitted in casual speech when the meaning is clear.


Level 01 Lesson 10 Lesson Notes

To say “to have” or “there is”:

있다 [it-tta] to exist / to have
있어요 [i-sseo-yo] there is / I have
Examples

시간 있어요? [si-gan i-sseo-yo?] Do you have time?
To say “to not have” or “there is not”:

없다 [eop-tta] to not exist / to not have

Examples

시간 없어요. [si-gan eop-sseo-yo.] I don’t have time.
친구 없어요. [chin-gu eop-sseo-yo.] I don’t have friends.
To emphasize a topic or subject:

시간은 없어요. [si-ga-neun eop-sseo-yo.] Time is what I don’t have.
시간이 없어요. [si-ga-ni eop-sseo-yo.] It’s time that I don’t have.
To say something is fun:

재미 [jae-mi] fun
재미있어요. [jae-mi-i-sseo-yo.] It’s fun. / It’s interesting.

Level 01 Lesson 11 Lesson Notes

To make a polite request, use:

주다 [ju-da] to give
주세요 [ju-se-yo] please give me...
Say the item first, then 주세요:

커피 주세요. [keo-pi ju-se-yo.] Please give me some coffee.
우유 주세요. [u-yu ju-se-yo.] Please give me some milk.
돈 주세요. [don ju-se-yo.] Please give me some money.
This phrase is used in cafés, shops, and many everyday situations, and means “give me, please,” not just “please.”

Level 01 Lesson 16 Lesson Notes

Korean verbs in the dictionary end in:

다 [da]
Examples:

가다 [ga-da] to go → 가 [ga]
먹다 [meok-tta] to eat → 먹 [meok]
보다 [bo-da] to see → 보 [bo]
보이다 [bo-i-da] to be seen → 보이 [bo-i]
하다 [ha-da] to do → 하 [ha]
Present tense endings:

-아요 [-a-yo] after ㅏ or ㅗ
-어요 [-eo-yo] after other vowels
-여요 [-yeo-yo] for 하다, becomes 해요
Examples:

가다 → 가요 [ga-yo] I go / you go / they go
먹다 → 먹어요 [meo-geo-yo] I eat / you eat / they eat
보다 → 봐요 [bwa-yo] I see / you see / they see
보이다 → 보여요 [bo-yeo-yo] it is seen / visible
하다 → 해요 [hae-yo] I do / you do / they do
To ask a question, raise the tone:

가요? [ga-yo?] Do you go?
먹어요? [meo-geo-yo?] Do you eat?
봐요? [bwa-yo?] Do you see?
보여요? [bo-yeo-yo?] Is it visible?
해요? [hae-yo?] Do you do?


Level 01 Lesson 17 Lesson notes

To conjugate verbs into the past tense, use:

-았어요 after ㅏ or ㅗ
-었어요 after other vowels
-였어요 for 하다 verbs (often becomes 했어요)
Examples:

사다 → 샀어요 (I bought)
오다 → 왔어요 (I came)
적다 → 적었어요 (I wrote down)
공부하다 → 공부했어요 (I studied)
Compare with present tense:

먹어요 → 먹었어요 (eat → ate)
잡아요 → 잡았어요 (catch → caught)
팔아요 → 팔았어요 (sell → sold)
놀아요 → 놀았어요 (play → played)
써요 → 썼어요 (use → used)
기다려요 → 기다렸어요 (wait → waited)
이상해요 → 이상했어요 (strange → was strange)
멋있어요 → 멋있었어요 (cool → was cool)
To make a question, raise your tone:

샀어요? (Did you buy it?)
놀았어요? (Did you hang out?)


Level 01 Lesson 18 Lesson Notes

To ask "where," use 어디.

어디 가고 싶어요? = Where do you want to go?
You can also say 어디에 가고 싶어요? — both are correct.
Use -에 after a place to mean “to,” “at,” or “in”:

학교에 갔어요 = I went to school
한국에 왔어요 = I came to Korea
어디에 있어요? = Where are you?
Note: -에 is often dropped in conversation when the meaning is clear.
Use -에서 to mean “from” or to describe where an action happens:

한국에서 왔어요 = I came from Korea
집에서 일해요 = I work at home
집에서 뭐 해요? = What are you doing at home?
Compare:

집에 있어요 = I’m at home
집에서 일해요 = I work at home

Level 01 Lesson 19 Lesson Notes

The Korean word for “when” is 언제. You can use 언제 on its own in a question, without any additional particles.

For example:

언제 했어요? = When did you do it?
언제 도착했어요? = When did you arrive?
언제 왔어요? = When did you come?
언제 일어나요? = When do you get up?
Note: 언제 is only used in questions. It doesn’t mean “at the time when…” as in “when you arrive” or “when I see you.”

You can also use 언제 as the subject:

언제가 좋아요? = When is good?
Here are some essential time-related words:

오늘 = today
어제 = yesterday
내일 = tomorrow
지금 = now
아까 = earlier today
나중에 = later
These words usually go before the verb but can move around more freely in Korean sentences than in English.

Level 01 Lesson 21 Lesson Notes

There are two common ways to make negative sentences in Korean.

Use 안 [an] before the verb (simple and casual)

집 [jib] house, home
집에 가요. [ji-be ga-yo] I’m going home.
집에 안 가요. [ji-be an ga-yo.] I’m not going home.
Example

그거 [geu-geo] it, that
버리다 [beo-ri-da] to throw away
그거 버렸어요. [geu-geo beo-ryeo-sseo-yo.] I threw that away.
그거 안 버렸어요. [geu-geo an beo-ryeo-sseo-yo.] I didn’t throw it away.
Use -지 않다 [-ji an-ta] (more formal or polite)

가다 [ga-da] to go
가지 않아요. [ga-ji a-na-yo.] I do not go.

Some verbs use a completely different word instead of following negative patterns:

있다 [it-tta] to exist → 없다 [eop-tta] to not exist
알다 [al-da] to know → 모르다 [mo-reu-da] to not know
These are the basics of how to make negative sentences in Korean.

To connect nouns or say “with” in Korean, use -하고 or -(이)랑.

Used after any noun, regardless of the final sound.

To say “also” or “too” in Korean, use the particle:

-도 [-do] also, too
Attach -도 directly to a noun or pronoun.

Examples:

저는 좋아요. [jeo-neun jo-a-yo.] As for me, I like it.
저도 좋아요. [jeo-do jo-a-yo.] I like it, too.
저 봤어요. [jeo bwa-sseo-yo.] I saw it.
저는 봤어요. [jeo-neun bwa-sseo-yo.] As for me, I saw it.
저도 봤어요. [jeo-do bwa-sseo-yo.] I saw it, too.
When replacing particles like -은/는, simply use -도:

저는 학생이에요. [jeo-neun hak-ssaeng-i-e-yo.] I am a student.
저도 학생이에요. [jeo-do hak-ssaeng-i-e-yo.] I am a student, too.
To say “this too”:

이것 가져왔어요. [i-geot ga-jeo-wa-sseo-yo.] I brought this.
이것도 가져왔어요. [i-geot-tto ga-jeo-wa-sseo-yo.] I brought this, too.
To say “today too”:

오늘 일해요? [o-neul il-hae-yo?] Do you work today?
오늘도 일해요? [o-neul-do il-hae-yo?] Do you work today too?
Meaning changes based on word placement:

저도 물 주세요. [jeo-do mul ju-se-yo.] Please give me water, too.
저 물도 주세요. [jeo mul-do ju-se-yo.] Please also give water to me.
Pay close attention to where -도 is attached to understand and control the nuance.

To say “can” or “be able to” in Korean, use:

자다 → 자야 되다 → 자야 돼요. [ja-ya dwae-yo.] I must sleep.
If the vowel is not ㅏ or ㅗ → -어야 되다

쓰다 → 써야 되다 → 써야 돼요. [sseo-ya dwae-yo.] I must write.
If the verb is 하다 → 해야 되다

하다 → 해야 되다 → 해야 돼요. [hae-ya dwae-yo.] I must do.
The key part that gives the meaning of “must” or “should” is:

-야 [-ya]
두 가지 동사 사용 가능:

되다 [doe-da]
하다 [ha-da]
가야 돼요. [ga-ya dwae-yo.] I have to go. (more casual)
가야 해요. [ga-ya hae-yo.] I must go. (more formal)
Example sentences:

집에 가야 돼요. [ji-be ga-ya dwae-yo.] I have to go home.
저는 뭐 해야 돼요? [jeo-neun mwo hae-ya dwae-yo?] What should I do?
언제까지 여기에 있어야 돼요? [eon-je-kka-ji yeo-gi-e i-sseo-ya dwae-yo?] Until when should I stay here?
누구한테 줘야 돼요? [nu-gu-han-te jwo-ya dwae-yo?] Who should I give this to?
어디에서 사야 돼요? [eo-di-e-seo sa-ya dwae-yo?] Where should I buy it?
Use these patterns to clearly express what must or should be done in Korean.

Level 02 Lesson 21 Lesson Notes

To say “more” in Korean, use:

더 [deo] more
Place 더 before adjectives, adverbs, or verbs.

빠르다 → 빨라요. [ppal-la-yo.] It’s fast
더 빨라요. [deo ppal-la-yo.] It’s faster.
비싸다 → 비싸요. [bi-ssa-yo.] It’s expensive.
더 비싸요. [deo bi-ssa-yo.] It’s more expensive.
예쁘다 → 예뻐요. [ye-ppeo-yo.] It’s pretty.
더 예뻐요. [deo ye-ppeo-yo.] It’s prettier.
To say “than” or “compared to,” use:

-보다 [-bo-da] than / compared to
Attach -보다 to the noun being compared.

사과보다 [sa-gwa-bo-da] than an apple
이것보다 [i-geot-bo-da] than this
Structure: [Noun + 보다] + 더 + [Adjective/Verb]

이것보다 더 커요. [i-geot-bo-da deo keo-yo.] It’s bigger than this.
수박은 사과보다 더 커요. [su-ba-geun sa-gwa-bo-da deo keo-yo.] Watermelons are bigger than apples.
영어는 한국어보다 더 어려워요. [yeong-eo-neun han-gu-geo-bo-da deo eo-ryeo-wo-yo.] English is more difficult than Korean.
오늘은 어제보다 더 더워요. [o-neu-reun eo-je-bo-da deo deo-wo-yo.] Today is hotter than yesterday.
Omitting 더:

오늘은 어제보다 더워요. [o-neu-reun eo-je-bo-da deo-wo-yo.] Today is hotter than yesterday.
Even without 더, the meaning is clear when context is known.
